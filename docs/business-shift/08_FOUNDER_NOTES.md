# 08. Founder Notes

**Prepared:** March 2026  
**For:** Ferdie Botden

## Direct View

You are probably looking at a real expansion of the Dominate offer, not a random special case.

The strongest insight is this:

- contractors may not resist ConversionOS
- they may resist **throwing away a site they already like**

That is a much more solvable problem than trying to convince them they should want a totally different front-end.

## What I Think You Are Missing

### 1. The objection is emotional, not just technical

Established contractors often see their site as part of their legitimacy. If they just paid for it, asking them to replace it feels wasteful even if your platform is better.

Your new wedge is:

> "Keep the feel. Upgrade the brain."

That is a cleaner message than "replace your site with ours."

### 2. This is probably an activation-heavy value proposition

The real work is front-loaded:

- audit
- rights clarification
- migration design
- content and asset handling
- cutover

That means the custom value shows up more in activation than in monthly recurring fees.

If Dominate includes this by default, you should still guard the activation economics very carefully.

### 3. Your biggest risk is agency creep

If you do not define a migration envelope, Dominate becomes:

- "whatever bespoke website request the client wants"

That will break margins, focus, and product discipline faster than the technical work itself.

## Recommended Positioning

The message I would lean into is:

> "For established contractors who like their current website, Dominate lets us preserve the feel of what is already working while moving you onto an AI-native conversion platform."

That is better than:

- "custom website with AI"
- "website copy with AI"
- "premium redesign"

Because it ties directly to:

- lower disruption
- preserved familiarity
- faster speed-to-lead
- richer lead context
- platform value after the lead comes in

## Pricing Cautions

You chose to include this inside Dominate. That can work, but only if Dominate has a clear envelope and an overage mechanism.

### My recommendation

- Keep standard Dominate at **$20,000 activation + $2,500/mo**
- Treat the standard migration envelope as included
- Add a **complexity addendum** when a site exceeds the envelope materially

Suggested internal framing:

- standard Dominate: included
- oversized/complex Dominate: addendum, likely in the **$5,000-$15,000** range depending on complexity
- beyond that: Black Label discussion

Without that, you will underprice complexity while telling yourself it is still "productized."

## What You Should Test with MD

MD is a good lighthouse case because it pressure-tests the exact objection:

- "we like our site"
- "we want your AI features"
- "we do not want a downgrade in look/feel"

What you should learn from MD:

- how much fidelity actually matters to the client
- which pages/interactions matter most to preserve
- whether the hero form / FAQ / blog depth really drive their trust perception
- whether the client values same-domain continuity enough to justify the premium motion

This should be treated as a commercial learning case, not just a design exercise.

## Strategic Opportunities You Should Not Miss

### 1. The "recent website spend" segment

There is a specific buyer profile here:

- recently redesigned site
- visually happy
- operationally underpowered

That is a sharper segment than generic contractor outreach.

### 2. Quarterly conversion reviews

If Dominate preserves the public site feel and powers the back-end, you have a natural premium ongoing service:

- quarterly CRO review
- CTA tuning
- form friction review
- mobile performance review
- lead-quality review

That fits your "revenue acceleration" positioning better than generic support.

### 3. A premium shell library

If you do this more than once, you should not think in terms of one-off websites. You should think in terms of:

- reusable shell patterns
- reusable contractor IA patterns
- reusable motion systems
- reusable page families

That is how you keep this from becoming a manual agency model.

## Risks to Watch

### 1. Scope bleed

The client says "keep the feel," then starts requesting:

- new pages
- custom calculators
- bigger blog migrations
- unique integrations
- endless rounds of visual revision

This must be caught early with scope rules.

### 2. Rights ambiguity

If the domain, images, or code situation is murky, the deal is riskier than it appears. Your first qualification question should increasingly be:

- "Who controls the domain and assets?"

not:

- "What is your budget?"

### 3. Internal inconsistency

Your own docs currently have pricing and feature inconsistencies. If you are going to sell a more sophisticated Dominate promise, your internal materials need to stop contradicting each other.

## What I Would Do Next

1. Use this dossier to align internal language.
2. Treat MD as the first commercial validation case for the preserve-and-upgrade story.
3. Add domain/IP control questions to the earliest sales stage.
4. Create a legal/contract addendum specifically for website migration and rights confirmation.
5. Keep Black Label separate and rare.
6. Once a first case proves out, convert repeated patterns into a future shell system rather than normalizing one-off custom work.

## Bottom Line

This direction looks promising because it solves a real objection without abandoning your platform thesis.

If you manage the boundaries, it strengthens Dominate.

If you do not manage the boundaries, it turns Dominate into an agency.
